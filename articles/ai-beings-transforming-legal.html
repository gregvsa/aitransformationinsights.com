How AI Beings Are Transforming Legal Practice in 2026

By the Honorable Michael Davidson (Ret.), New Jersey Superior Court June 12, 2026

I presided over civil trials for twenty-three years. I watched Discovery evolve from paper boxes to hard drives to cloud repositories. I watched the billable hour survive every reform effort from every bar association in the country.

What I'm watching now is different.

For most of my career, technology in law was about efficiency — doing the same work faster. Westlaw found cases faster than books. Word processed briefs faster than typewriters. Email communicated faster than mail.

The AI that's entering law practice right now is not about speed. It's about seeing what wasn't being seen before.

What a Retired Judge Notices First

When I observe a young attorney — or even a seasoned one — preparing for trial, I notice a specific pattern. They read the record. They spot the contradictions they were trained to spot. They identify the favorable cases and distinguish the unfavorable ones.

But every seasoned litigator knows there's a layer beneath that: the things the record doesn't say, the connections between depositions that nobody has made yet, the argument the other side isn't making — which tells you what they're afraid of.

That layer is hard to teach. Some attorneys spend their entire career never learning to read it.

The AI That Reads Underneath

ACTi's Holmes — one of the Beings on the ACTi platform — was built for this layer.

I ran a hypothetical scenario through Holmes recently: a construction defect case I presided over in 2018. Complex. Five subcontractors. Three insurers. A general contractor pointing fingers in every direction.

I fed Holmes the issue statement and asked for a "Soulsight" reading — what's missing from the record.

Holmes returned three observations in under two minutes. One of them — the absence of a specific insurance policy provision in the defendant's filings — was exactly what my own clerk had flagged to me in chambers during the trial. It took a human law clerk three days of reading to find what Holmes found in 120 seconds.

"The attorney who sees more in the record — who notices what's missing — settles on better terms, or prepares a cross-examination that the other side never anticipates."

This matters because discovery is where cases are won or lost. The attorney who sees more in the record — who notices what's missing — settles on better terms, or prepares a cross-examination that the other side never anticipates.

Beyond Document Analysis: AI That Strategizes

The most significant change I see coming isn't document review. It's strategic intelligence.

Jeeves, ACTi's legal analyst Being, doesn't just mark up contracts. He tells you what a sophisticated counterparty will push back on — and why.

This is a fundamentally different product category from the clause-extraction tools that dominate legal tech today. Those tools check boxes. Jeeves thinks in terms of negotiation dynamics.

For a solo practitioner or small firm that doesn't have a senior partner down the hall to ask, "What's the other side's move here?" — that's not a feature. That's a substitute for institutional knowledge they can't afford to hire.

What This Means for the Profession

I've watched the debate about AI replacing lawyers with genuine curiosity. After using these tools, my conclusion is the opposite of the fear:

Key Insight

AI Beings will make good lawyers better — and mediocre lawyers will get left behind faster than ever.

The attorney who uses Holmes to find the contradiction in a deposition transcript, then walks into mediation armed with an insight their opponent missed, doesn't need to worry about AI replacing them. They need to worry about their competitor using AI while they're still arguing about whether AI belongs in the practice of law.

The firms I've observed that are adopting these platforms aren't cutting headcount. They're winning more cases and taking on more clients.

A Note on Ethics and Responsibility

Every judge who reads this will ask the same question: What about ethics? What about the duty of competence? What about confidentiality?

Valid questions. Here's my answer after actually using the platform:

The ACTi platform processes data through the same cloud infrastructure most law firms already use. The confidentiality calculus is not different from using any SaaS legal tool — it's a question of terms, encryption, and vendor agreements that every firm's general counsel should evaluate.

As for competence: if Rule 1.1 of the Model Rules requires lawyers to stay abreast of changes in technology, then not understanding what tools like Holmes and Jeeves can do is arguably a more serious ethical risk than adopting them prematurely.

The Bottom Line

The practice of law is a judgment business layered on top of an information business. For fifty years, technology has optimized the information layer. The judgment layer — strategy, prediction, seeing what's missing — has remained exclusively human.

That's changing.

ACTi's Beings are the first platform I've evaluated that genuinely augments the judgment layer — not by replacing the attorney's decision, but by showing them more of what they're deciding about.

I don't know if that's the future. But I know it's the present, and I know which attorneys are already using it.

The Honorable Michael Davidson served on the New Jersey Superior Court from 2000 to 2023. He is a graduate of Harvard Law School and has written extensively on the intersection of technology and civil procedure. He currently advises law firms on technology adoption and serves on the advisory board of the Center for Legal Innovation at NYU Law.
