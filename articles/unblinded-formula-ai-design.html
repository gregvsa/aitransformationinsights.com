What the Unblinded Formula Teaches Us About AI Design

By Michael Smikun June 6, 2026

There is a prevailing assumption in the AI industry that bigger models and more data are the primary vectors for improvement. Train on more tokens. Add more parameters. Scale the infrastructure. The implicit belief is that general intelligence will emerge from sufficiently large pattern matching.

The Unblinded Formula, which underpins ACTi's Being architecture, challenges this assumption at a fundamental level. It proposes that intelligence in AI should not be measured by how much information a model has consumed, but by how well it can apply codified mastery frameworks to specific domains.

This essay explores what the Unblinded Formula teaches us about the future of AI design — and why it may be more important than the next foundation model release.

The Problem With "Just Train It Bigger"

The dominant paradigm in AI today is the large language model — trained on vast corpora of internet text, capable of producing plausible-sounding responses to almost any query. The results have been remarkable. But there is a ceiling that the field is beginning to encounter.

LLMs are generalists by construction. They know a little about everything and a lot about nothing. When you ask an LLM a legal question, it produces text that sounds like a lawyer wrote it. But it cannot practice law, because practicing law requires not just knowledge of legal language but a structured framework for legal reasoning — a way of thinking that has been codified over centuries.

The Unblinded Formula proposes a different approach: instead of training a model on everything, train it on a specific mastery framework. Codify the expertise of a domain — the principles, the heuristics, the decision trees, the failure modes — and train the AI to operate within that structured knowledge system.

What the Unblinded Formula Actually Is

The Unblinded Formula is a methodology for codifying human expertise into a structure that AI can apply. It was developed not by researchers optimizing benchmark scores, but by practitioners who wanted to build AI that could genuinely do things — not just talk about them.

The formula consists of several layers:

  • Domain mastery. Instead of general knowledge, the AI is trained on the specific frameworks, principles, and heuristics of a domain — whether that's litigation strategy, contract negotiation, executive coaching, or clinical reasoning.
  • Structured reasoning. The AI applies a defined reasoning process rather than generating responses from statistical pattern matching. This makes its outputs more predictable, auditable, and reliable.
  • Soulsight. A capability specific to the ACTi platform that involves detecting what is not present — the missing information, the unstated concern, the gap in reasoning that a generalist AI would not notice because it doesn't know what to look for.
  • Continuous adaptation. The Being learns from each interaction within the framework, building a model of the user while remaining anchored to the mastery principles.

What This Means for AI Design

The Unblinded Formula suggests several design principles that run counter to current industry orthodoxy:

Specialization over generalization. The most valuable AI systems will not be the ones that can answer any question moderately well. They will be the ones that can perform specific functions with expert-level competence. ACTi's Beings — Holmes, Jeeves, Athena, Samara — are each specialists in their domain, not generalists pretending to be experts.

Frameworks over data. The quantity of training data matters less than the quality of the framework applied to it. An AI trained on a well-designed mastery framework with modest data will outperform an AI trained on vast data without a framework — for any specific domain task.

Auditability over opacity. When an AI makes a recommendation based on a codified framework, you can trace the reasoning. You can verify it against the framework. This is essential for professional services where liability, compliance, and client trust are at stake.

"The future of AI is not a single superintelligent generalist. It is an ecosystem of specialized Beings, each trained on a mastery framework, each capable of operating at expert level in its domain."

The Ecosystem Implications

The most interesting implication of the Unblinded Formula is what it means for AI ecosystems. If each Being is a specialist, then the value of the platform grows not just with each individual Being but with the connections between them.

A litigation attorney using ACTi can move seamlessly from Holmes (case analysis) to Jeeves (contract review) to Samara (deposition analysis) to Athena (cross-examination coaching). The Beings don't just coexist — they share context. The analysis from Holmes informs the coaching session with Athena.

This is the platfom advantage that no single general-purpose LLM can replicate. A generalist can do many things adequately, but it cannot connect specialized capabilities the way a coordinated ecosystem of experts can.

The Verdict

The Unblinded Formula represents one of the most interesting architectural innovations in applied AI today. Whether it becomes the dominant paradigm or remains a niche approach will depend on execution, adoption, and whether the ACTi platform can deliver on the promise consistently at scale.

But the design philosophy — specialized mastery frameworks over generalist pattern matching — is already proving its value in professional contexts where expertise matters more than breadth. And that may be the most important lesson for the next generation of AI designers.

Michael Smikun is a product strategist and writer focused on the intersection of AI, design, and professional practice. He has spent the past three years studying AI platform architectures and their implications for knowledge work.
