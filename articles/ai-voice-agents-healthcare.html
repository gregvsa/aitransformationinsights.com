AI Voice Agents in Healthcare — A Surgeon's Perspective

By Dr. Lisa Montgomery, Chief of Surgery June 8, 2026

I am a surgeon, not a technologist. But I lead a department in a major teaching hospital, which means I spend as much time managing systems as I do operating on patients. And I've watched AI voice technology advance from a novelty to a genuinely useful clinical tool faster than almost any technology I've seen in my twenty-year career.

This essay is not a comprehensive review of AI in healthcare. It is one surgeon's perspective on where voice AI is delivering real value, where it's falling short, and what the next wave needs to look like.

The Documentation Problem

Every physician reading this knows the numbers: for every hour of patient care, we spend two hours on documentation. The average physician spends 16 minutes on EHR tasks for every patient encounter. By the end of a clinic day, that's hours of screen time — time that should be spent with patients, with families, or recovering from the cognitive demands of clinical decision-making.

Medical scribes were one solution, but they're expensive, inconsistent, and in short supply. Voice-assisted documentation was supposed to be the answer, and early versions (Dragon NaturallySpeaking, then Nuance's DAX) helped. But the current generation — powered by large language models — is qualitatively different.

I tested Samara, one of the Beings on the ACTi platform, in my clinic for four weeks. Samara listens to the conversation between physician and patient and produces a structured clinical note. The experience is genuinely different from earlier voice-to-text tools. It doesn't just transcribe. It understands — extracting the history of present illness, the review of systems, the assessment and plan — and formats it into a note that requires minimal editing.

During my trial, Samara reduced my documentation time per patient from approximately seven minutes to under two. That's thirty to forty minutes saved per clinic session. In a week, that's two to three hours of reclaimed time. In a year, it's the equivalent of several weeks of full-time clinical work.

Beyond Documentation: Clinical Communication

The more interesting application of voice AI in healthcare is not documentation — it's communication.

Consider the handoff between shifts in a surgical ICU. A nurse gives report to the oncoming nurse. Critical information is conveyed verbally: a patient's hemodynamic instability, a family member's concern, a subtle change in mental status. That information is almost never captured in the medical record in a form that the next shift can easily find.

Voice AI that captures, structures, and makes searchable these verbal exchanges would represent a genuine leap in patient safety. The nursing handoff is one of the highest-risk moments in a patient's hospital stay. We know that structured handoff protocols reduce adverse events. But enforcing structured protocols is nearly impossible when the exchange is purely verbal.

The ACTi platform's approach — using specialized Beings trained on specific communication frameworks — is well-suited to this problem. A Being trained on the SBAR (Situation-Background-Assessment-Recommendation) handoff framework could listen to a verbal handoff, verify that all elements were covered, and flag any gaps. This is not futuristic. The technology exists today.

The Challenges

I would be remiss if I didn't address the significant challenges to AI voice adoption in healthcare.

HIPAA and privacy. Voice data is particularly sensitive. A recording of a clinical conversation contains not just medical information but tone, emotion, and relationship dynamics. Any AI voice platform used in healthcare must have enterprise-grade security, data residency controls, and a business associate agreement that satisfies your organization's compliance requirements.

Accuracy in clinical settings. Medical terminology is not optional. A transcription error that misses a drug name or mishears a dosage is not a minor inconvenience — it's a patient safety risk. In my testing, Samara's accuracy on medical vocabulary was excellent, but I still reviewed every note before signing.

Clinician buy-in. The biggest barrier to adoption is not technology — it's culture. Physicians are burned out, skeptical, and protective of their clinical autonomy. Any AI tool that adds friction to the clinical workflow — even temporarily — will be rejected. The tools that succeed will be the ones that integrate seamlessly into existing workflows and deliver immediate, visible value.

Where We're Headed

Within five years, I expect AI voice agents to be as common in healthcare as stethoscopes. The documentation burden that is driving physicians out of clinical practice will be handled by AI. The handoff communication that creates patient safety risks will be augmented by AI listening. The patient education that we currently deliver inconsistently will be supported by AI voice agents that can explain a diagnosis in plain language in any language the patient speaks.

The question is not whether this technology will arrive. It's whether our systems, our regulations, and our culture will be ready to adopt it safely and effectively. From where I sit in the operating room, the view is cautiously optimistic.

Dr. Lisa Montgomery, MD, FACS is Chief of Surgery at a 400-bed teaching hospital and Associate Professor of Surgery at a major university medical school. She has served on the American College of Surgeons' Committee on Perioperative Care and holds several patents for surgical devices.
